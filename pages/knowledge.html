Frequently Asked

Questions that deserve real answers

The most-asked doubts about Gau Seva, indigenous breeds and natural farming — answered clearly, without jargon.

01 Why is cow protection so important for Bharat?

The cow has been the ecological, economic and spiritual anchor of Bharat for thousands of years. Her milk, ghee and curd nourish families; her dung and urine regenerate soil and drive natural farming; and her presence sustains rural livelihoods across every state.

Protecting Gau Mata is therefore not a religious act alone — it is protecting Bharat's food security, ecology, cultural identity and dharma.

02 What is the difference between Indian indigenous breeds and foreign breeds?

Indigenous breeds like Gir, Sahiwal, Tharparkar, Rathi and Kankrej carry a characteristic hump, dewlap and long ears — they are perfectly adapted to Bharat's climate, produce nutrient-rich A2 milk and thrive on local fodder.

Exotic breeds are bred primarily for high milk yield but produce A1 milk, require intensive management, imported feed, and struggle in Indian conditions. Reviving desi breeds means healthier milk, resilient farms and independence from imports.

03 What exactly is Panchagavya and how is it used?

Panchagavya literally means "five products of the cow" — milk, curd, ghee, dung and urine — combined in specific proportions and fermented for 7–15 days.

It is used in Ayurveda as a health tonic, in agriculture as a bio-stimulant that boosts plant growth and disease resistance, and in traditional worship as a purifying substance. Modern research shows it enriches microbial life in both soil and gut.

04 How does cow-based organic farming work in practice?

Gau-based natural farming (also known as ZBNF or Subhash Palekar Krishi) uses four core inputs made from indigenous cow dung and urine: jeevamruth (soil inoculant), bijamruth (seed treatment), acchadana (mulching) and whapasa (moisture management).

One desi cow can support up to 30 acres of chemical-free farmland — dramatically cutting input costs while regenerating soil, water and biodiversity.

05 Can a cow-based rural economy really uplift villages?

Yes — a single indigenous cow generates multiple income streams for a farming family: A2 milk and dairy products, organic manure and bio-fertilizer for sale, dung-cakes as cooking fuel, and traction power for tilling.

When scaled across a village with gau-based farming, panchagavya units and dairy co-operatives, this creates dignified, low-cost livelihoods that do not require migration or debt — the very foundation of a self-reliant Bharat.

06 How can I contribute if I am not a farmer or scholar?

Every citizen has a role. You can share verified knowledge from this Hub with your network, sponsor fodder for a gaushala for a month, choose A2 milk from indigenous breeds, attend a satsang, or join a district volunteer chapter.

Even two hours a week of committed seva makes a meaningful difference — dharma doesn't need a title, only sincerity.
